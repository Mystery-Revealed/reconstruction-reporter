# Reconstruction Reporter — Content Draft (Fable pass)
### All student-facing text · 6 assignments × 2 decisions = 12 graded actions
*Meters: **Truth** 📰 · **Sources** 🤝 · **Standing** 🏛️ — all start at 50.*
*Verdicts: ✅ right (1 pt) · ⚠️ partial (0.5) · ❌ wrong (0). Meter numbers are a first draft — the Opus build pass balances them with a simulation so all-right lands in the top tier.*

## Title Screen

**RECONSTRUCTION REPORTER**
*Galveston, Texas · 1865–1876*

Your editor hands you a notebook and the biggest story in Texas: what freedom means, who is fighting it, and what this state becomes next. Get the facts. Protect the people who trust you with them. Print the truth — even when it costs.

**Button:** Start Reporting

**How to play (rules card):**
You are a young reporter for a Galveston newspaper. Each assignment gives you two decisions: what to cover, and how to check your sources. Watch three meters:
- **Truth 📰** — how accurate your printed record is.
- **Sources 🤝** — how much people trust you. Some sources risk everything to talk to you.
- **Standing 🏛️** — your paper's reputation and your job.
These meters pull against each other. That is the point. Printing hard truths can cost you Standing. Protecting a source can too. A good reporter pays that price.

## Assignment 1 — The Day of Jubilee
**June 19, 1865**

> General Gordon Granger has landed in Galveston. Today he read an order out loud: every enslaved person in Texas is free. That is about 250,000 people. The streets are filling with song and tears of joy. Your editor wants the story by dark.

### Decision 1 — What do you cover?
| | Choice | Effects | Feedback |
|---|---|---|---|
| ✅ | **Go where the story is. Interview freedpeople themselves about what this day means.** | Truth +10, Sources +10, Standing +5 | "The people at the center of the story *are* the story. Their words made the record that matters." |
| ⚠️ | **Interview the general's staff and copy down the order.** | Truth +5 | "Accurate — and hollow. An order on paper is not a day of jubilee." |
| ❌ | **Write it from the office. Everyone already knows what happened.** | Truth −10 | "Nobody knew what happened until someone asked. That's the job." |

### Decision 2 — Source check
> On your way back, a planter stops you. He says the freedpeople "won't work without masters." He wants that in the paper. Do you print it?

| | Choice | Effects | Feedback |
|---|---|---|---|
| ✅ | **Note it as *his claim*. Then find out what freedpeople and Freedmen's Bureau agents actually report — and print the evidence.** | Truth +10 | "He has a stake in the story — he lost the labor he never paid for. A source with something to gain needs backing up. That's called *corroboration*: checking a claim against other proof. (Skills: 7.20D/F)" |
| ⚠️ | **Print his quote with no check.** | Standing +5, Truth −10 | "His words are real. But printing a claim without checking it makes it look true. Your readers deserve the check." |
| ❌ | **Print it as fact, in your own voice.** | Truth −15 | "Now *his* opinion wears *your* paper's name. That's how a false idea becomes 'common knowledge.'" |

## Assignment 2 — The School
**1866 · A Freedmen's Bureau school opens**

> The Freedmen's Bureau — the government office set up to help freedpeople — has opened a school near the wharf. The room is packed: children, parents, even grandparents at the slates. Many walked past angry stares to get here. Learning to read was once against the law for them. Not anymore.

### Decision 1 — What do you cover?
| | Choice | Effects | Feedback |
|---|---|---|---|
| ✅ | **Sit in the classroom. Talk to the teacher and the students. Show the hunger to learn.** | Truth +10, Sources +10, Standing +5 | "One room, every age, all learning at once — that picture tells Texas what freedom is *for*. You printed it." |
| ⚠️ | **Report that a school opened, using the Bureau's official notice.** | Truth +5 | "True, but thin. A notice can't show you a grandmother learning her letters beside her grandson." |
| ❌ | **Skip it. 'A school opening isn't news.'** | Truth −10, Sources −5 | "For people once forbidden to read, a school is the biggest news there is. You missed the story of the year." |

### Decision 2 — Source check
> A rumor is moving through town: "The Bureau steals the workers' wages." A man repeats it to you like it's fact. Do you print it?

| | Choice | Effects | Feedback |
|---|---|---|---|
| ✅ | **Ask the Bureau to open its contract ledgers. Compare the rumor to the records. Print what the documents show.** | Truth +10, Sources +5 | "Documents beat rumor. The ledgers are a *primary source* — a record made at the time, by the people involved. (Skills: 7.20D/F)" |
| ⚠️ | **Print 'some say the Bureau takes wages' without checking.** | Standing +5, Truth −10 | "'Some say' is how a rumor sneaks into print wearing a suit. You gave it a ride." |
| ❌ | **Print the rumor as fact.** | Truth −15 | "A rumor printed becomes a 'fact' quoted. Papers that don't check become megaphones for whoever shouts first." |

## Assignment 3 — The Black Codes ⚠️ *(sensitivity-reviewed)*
**1866 · The legislature passes new laws**

> The Texas legislature has passed new laws about freedpeople. People are calling them the Black Codes. A politician tells you they are "fair labor laws that help everyone." Your editor drops the statute book on your desk. (A *statute* is the actual written law.)

### Decision 1 — What do you report?
| | Choice | Effects | Feedback |
|---|---|---|---|
| ✅ | **Read the law yourself. Say plainly what it does: it forces freedpeople into labor contracts, limits where they can travel and work, and blocks their testimony in court against white Texans.** | Truth +15, Standing −5 | "You named it. These laws were written to keep freedpeople as close to slavery as the law would allow — slavery's shadow, in statute form. Saying so plainly cost your paper some friends. It bought the record the truth." |
| ⚠️ | **Summarize the politician's version, but add a line of your own doubts.** | Truth +5 | "A doubt whispered next to a claim shouted — readers hear the shout. Half the truth is a hard way to be half right." |
| ❌ | **Print the politician's 'explainer' as your story.** | Truth −15, Standing +5 | "He wrote the law. Now he wrote your story too. The paper gained a friend in office and lost its reason to exist." |

### Decision 2 — Source check
> The politician offers you his written "explainer" of the new laws. It says they simply "encourage honest work."

| | Choice | Effects | Feedback |
|---|---|---|---|
| ✅ | **Put his words next to the law's actual text, side by side. Let readers see both.** | Truth +10 | "The statute is the primary source — his explainer is spin. When a summary and the real text disagree, the real text wins. (Skills: 7.20D/F)" |
| ⚠️ | **Ask him sharp follow-up questions, but never open the statute yourself.** | Truth +5 | "Good questions, wrong source. He can dodge a question. He can't dodge his own law's words — but only if you read them." |
| ❌ | **Take his word for it. He wrote the law — he'd know.** | Truth −10 | "He'd know best *and* has the most reason to shade it. The more a source stands to gain, the harder you check." |

## Assignment 4 — Night Riders ⚠️ *(sensitivity-reviewed — strictest treatment)*
**1868 · Terror comes at night**

> Hooded men have been riding at night in the county — leaving threats at the doors of freedpeople who vote, teach, or farm their own land. Fence posts burned. Warnings nailed to a church door. A farmer — a freedman respected in his church — agrees to tell you what he has seen. On one condition: **his name stays out of the paper.** If it's printed, the riders will come for him.

### Decision 1 — The witness
| | Choice | Effects | Feedback |
|---|---|---|---|
| ✅ | **Protect his name. Then gather more: other witnesses, the burned posts, the Bureau's reports. Print the *pattern* with the evidence.** | Truth +10, Sources +15, Standing −5 | "He risked everything to talk. You protected him *and* printed the truth — not one man's story, but a pattern proven many ways. Courage and care can work together." |
| ⚠️ | **Print the story with his name. 'Readers trust a named source.'** | Truth +5, Sources −15 | "The story ran. And the man who trusted you is now in danger — and no one else will ever talk to you. Some prices are not yours to spend." |
| ❌ | **Drop the story. It's too dangerous.** | Truth −10, Sources −5 | "Silence is what the riders are counting on. Terror works best in the dark. There were careful, safe ways to bring it into the light — and you had them." |

### Decision 2 — Source check
> You ask the sheriff about the night riders. He waves you off: "Exaggeration. A few pranks." Now you must weigh a threatened witness against an official denial.

| | Choice | Effects | Feedback |
|---|---|---|---|
| ✅ | **Weigh what each source has at stake. Corroborate carefully — witnesses, Bureau records, the physical evidence. Print the evidence, and note the sheriff's denial as *his claim*.** | Truth +10, Standing −5 | "A badge doesn't make a claim true, and fear doesn't make a witness false. You weighed *evidence*, not titles. That's the skill. (Skills: 7.20D/F)" |
| ⚠️ | **Print both statements side by side and let readers decide — without weighing the evidence.** | Standing +5, Truth −5 | "'Both sides' sounds fair. But one side had proof and one had a shrug — and your story hid the difference. Fairness to sources is not fairness to facts." |
| ❌ | **Take the sheriff's word. Kill the story.** | Truth −15, Sources −10 | "The official denial was the easy exit, and you took it. The record now says 'nothing happened.' The people it happened to know better — and they know your paper now too." |

**Scripted event after Assignment 4 (all players, not graded):** *Angry letters pour in. Two advertisers pull out.* **Standing −10.** — "Telling the truth about the riders has a price in 1868 Texas. You are paying it. So did the real editors who did this."

## Assignment 5 — New Voters
**1869–1873 · Black Texans vote and hold office**

> Under the new state constitution, Black men in Texas vote for the first time — and win office. In the capitol, Senators **Matt Gaines** and **George T. Ruby** — two of the first Black state senators in Texas history — are at work on schools, railroads, and laws to protect voters.

### Decision 1 — What do you cover?
| | Choice | Effects | Feedback |
|---|---|---|---|
| ✅ | **Go to the capitol. Report what Gaines and Ruby actually say and do — the bills, the speeches, the votes.** | Truth +10, Sources +5, Standing +5 | "History was in session and you were in the room. Gaines fought for public schools; Ruby built political power on the docks of Galveston. The record has their words because someone wrote them down." |
| ⚠️ | **Report the election results, but skip covering the men at work.** | Truth +5 | "You recorded *that* it happened, not *what* it meant. Numbers without faces are easy to forget — and easy to dismiss." |
| ❌ | **Skip it for a cotton-prices story.** | Truth −10 | "Cotton prices repeat every year. The first Black senators in Texas history happen once. You covered the wrong first." |

### Decision 2 — Source check
> A rival paper runs a column calling the new legislature "ignorant men playing at government." Your editor asks what you make of it.

| | Choice | Effects | Feedback |
|---|---|---|---|
| ✅ | **Spot the loaded words. Check the claim against the record — the bills passed, the schools funded, the speeches given — and answer it with evidence.** | Truth +10 | "'Ignorant' and 'playing' are *loaded words* — words picked to make you feel a judgment before you've seen a fact. Loaded words tell you about the writer, not the subject. (Skill: 7.20D — detecting bias)" |
| ⚠️ | **Reprint the column with a note that 'views differ.'** | Standing +5, Truth −5 | "'Views differ' is not a check. You handed their megaphone a second stage and called it balance." |
| ❌ | **Echo the same words in your own column.** | Truth −15 | "Their bias just became your bias, in your voice, under your paper's name. This is how loaded words travel — one lazy echo at a time." |

## Assignment 6 — The 1876 Constitution
**1876 · Reconstruction ends**

> The soldiers are gone. The men who call themselves "Redeemers" — Texans who fought to roll back Reconstruction — have written a new state constitution. It cuts the governor's power, shortens terms, and slashes money for schools. Voters approve it. Texas still lives under this constitution today. Your editor wants two things: the news, and — because you've earned it — a farewell column on the whole decade.

### Decision 1 — The news story
| | Choice | Effects | Feedback |
|---|---|---|---|
| ✅ | **Report what the document actually changes — weaker government, shorter terms, school cuts — and who gains and loses by it.** | Truth +10 | "You read it and reported it: a constitution built on distrust of government. Texans still amend it today, hundreds of times over — because you can't run a modern state on an 1876 document without patching it." |
| ⚠️ | **Report that a new constitution passed, with quotes from its backers.** | Truth +5, Standing +5 | "You printed the *event* and their *spin*, but never opened the document. The backers thank you. The record doesn't." |
| ❌ | **Print the celebration story your publisher wants.** | Truth −10, Standing +10 | "The publisher is pleased. The story is a press release. Somewhere in the last decade, that stopped being your kind of journalism — didn't it?" |

### Decision 2 — Your farewell column *(the closing choice)*
> Eleven years. Jubilee, schools, statutes, night riders, senators, and now this. What does your column say the decade was?

| | Choice | Effects | Feedback |
|---|---|---|---|
| ✅ | **Tell the decade honestly — gains and losses both. Freedom proclaimed. Schools built against the odds. Votes cast and offices won. Laws and terror that fought all of it. And a constitution that rolled much of it back.** | Truth +15, Sources +5, Standing −5 | "Both things are true, and you printed both. The gains were real. The rollback was real. A record that keeps both is the only record worth keeping — and yours will still be telling the truth in a hundred years." |
| ⚠️ | **Write only the hopeful parts.** | Truth +5, Standing +5 | "Every word true, and the story still false. Leaving out the losses doesn't protect the gains — it just leaves readers unarmed for what comes next." |
| ❌ | **Write that Reconstruction was 'a mistake best forgotten' — the Redeemers' version.** | Truth −15, Standing +5 | "For almost a century, that version *was* the record in many books. It was wrong then. Your notebook knew better — you watched the schools fill and the senators work. You just chose not to say so." |

**Scripted event after Assignment 6 (all players, not graded):** *The era turns. Papers that told hard truths feel the chill.* **Standing −5.**

## Endings

Ending = **sum of the three meters** → tier. *(Thresholds below are draft targets — Opus balances them via simulation: all-right → top, all-partial → middle, all-wrong → bottom.)*

### 🏆 "The Record Stands" *(top tier, draft ≥ 200)*
You protected your sources, checked every claim, and printed the truth with the bill attached. Your paper lost some friends and kept its soul. A century and a half later, historians will lean on records like yours to tell this era straight. The record stands — because you did.

### 🖋️ "Ink and Nerve" *(middle tier, draft ≥ 110)*
You got much of the story, and you paid for some of it. A few claims slipped through unchecked; a few hard truths came out softer than they should have. But your notebook holds real history, honestly gathered. Reporters like you kept the record alive when it would have been easier to let it go dark.

### 📰 "The Story Got Away" *(bottom tier, draft < 110)*
The rumors got printed. The spin got through. The hardest stories died quietly in your notebook. The history still happened — but your readers never learned it from you, and the false version got a head start it would keep for decades. The story of this decade deserved a better witness. Play again — it's waiting.

### Debrief (all tiers — "what the record shows, 150 years later")
- **Juneteenth lived.** The day Granger read the order in Galveston became a Texas holiday in 1980 — and a **national holiday in 2021**. The people you interviewed on that first day started something the whole country now keeps.
- **The 1876 Constitution still governs Texas.** Built on distrust of government, it has been amended over 500 times — Texans are still voting on patches to it in every election.
- **The era's unfinished business didn't stay buried.** The rights won and rolled back in Reconstruction — voting, schools, equal standing in court — become the heart of the civil-rights movement you'll meet in **Unit 7**. The freedpeople's fight in this game is chapter one of that story.
- **And the skills you used are real.** Weighing loaded words, checking claims against documents, protecting sources, corroborating before printing — that's how real journalists and real historians still separate the record from the noise. (TEKS 7.5C · 7.20D/F · 7.16B)

## Balance notes for the Opus pass
- All-right path (with caps at 100): Truth caps at 100, Sources ≈ 100, Standing ≈ 30–35 after the two scripted hits → sum ≈ 230 → top tier at ≥200 works as drafted, but **verify by simulation** (all-right / all-partial / all-wrong) like Empresario.
- Standing is *designed* to end low even on perfect play — the era punished truth-tellers; the debrief says so. No fail state (mirror Empresario: no failCheck/failEnding).
- Scripted events: after A4 (Standing −10) and after A6 (Standing −5), unconditional, with one-line explanations shown to the player.

## Sensitivity self-check (per Common Standards §9 + spec §6)
- **A3:** the Codes are named plainly ("slavery's shadow, in statute form") at headline level; grading rewards *naming* the harm, and feedback never softens it. Accuracy-≠-endorsement rule not needed here since no choice requires enacting harm.
- **A4:** violence conveyed by threat, testimony, and pattern only — burned fence posts, warnings on a door, "the riders will come for him." No attack depicted or described. The witness has a church, a farm, standing, and courage — agency, not victimhood. The ⚠️ feedback treats endangering him as the serious wrong it is, without describing consequences graphically.
- **Freedpeople as protagonists throughout:** interviewed at Jubilee, learning at the school, testifying with courage, voting, legislating (Gaines and Ruby named and credited).
- **Honest arc:** the ending doesn't pretend the era ended well; the debrief bends toward the long record (Juneteenth) without erasing the rollback.
