# "Reconstruction Reporter" — Build Specification
### Unit 5 Game · Reconstruction · *Common Build Standards apply (Pattern A — Engine Game)*

## 1. At a Glance
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **TEKS** | 7.5C (political/economic/social effects of the Civil War and Reconstruction), 7.20D/F (bias, source validity — built into play), 7.16B (free press) |
| **Role** | A young reporter for a Galveston newspaper, 1865–1876 (everyone plays the reporter — class-wide group) |
| **Type** | Solo role game — 6 assignments × 2 decisions = **12 graded actions**; each assignment pairs a reporting decision with a **source/bias check** |
| **Adapter** | `reconstructionReporter.js` · meters `{ truth, sources, standing }` start 50 |

**Pitch:** Your editor hands you a notebook and the biggest story in Texas: what freedom means, who's fighting it, and what this state becomes next. Get the facts. Protect the people who trust you with them. Print the truth even when it costs.

**Teaching idea:** Reconstruction is where 7.5C's *effects* and 7.20's *skills* meet naturally — a reporter must witness Juneteenth, the Freedmen's Bureau, Black Codes, Klan terror, and the 1876 Constitution **and** learn to weigh loaded words, self-interested witnesses, and rumor against evidence. Every assignment grades both what you cover and *how you verify it*. This is the unit's most important — and most sensitive — game; the standards' rules (dignity, honesty at headline level, no graphic violence) govern every line.

## 2. Content Bank (your outline's Reconstruction beats)
**Juneteenth (June 19, 1865):** Granger at Galveston; freedom proclaimed for 250,000 enslaved Texans · **Freedmen's Bureau:** food, contracts, hospitals, and above all **schools**; Black literacy blooms against violent resistance · **Black Codes (1866):** Texas laws restricting freedpeople's work, movement, testimony — slavery's shadow in statute form · **the Klan and terror:** organized violence against Black Texans and their allies; witnesses risk everything (headline-level treatment) · **military Reconstruction (1867+):** Texas under Army oversight; new constitution, Black men vote and hold office (name **Matt Gaines** and **George T. Ruby**, Black state senators) · **the "Redeemers" and the 1876 Constitution:** Reconstruction rolled back; a constitution built on distrust of government — the one Texas still amends today · Galveston as boomtown backdrop (the era's biggest Texas city and port).

## 3. Mechanics
Meters: **Truth** 📰 (accuracy of the printed record), **Sources** 🤝 (trust of the people who talk to you — especially freedpeople risking much), **Standing** 🏛️ (your paper's credibility and your job). Tension by design: protecting sources sometimes costs Standing; printing hard truths costs Standing but builds Truth — the *right* answers favor truth and source safety, teaching journalistic ethics inside history. Ending = sum → tiers ("The Record Stands" / "Ink and Nerve" / "The Story Got Away") + debrief: what the record shows a century-and-a-half later — Juneteenth becomes a national holiday; the 1876 constitution still governs Texas; the era's unfinished business feeds Unit 7's civil-rights movement.

## 4. Sample Scripted Phase + Beat Matrix
**Assignment 1 — June 19, 1865 (decision 1):** *General Granger's order is read in Galveston. The streets fill with joy. Your editor wants the story by dark.*
- ✅ **Go where the story is — interview freedpeople themselves about what this day means.** Truth +10, Sources +10. *"The people at the center of the story are the story. Their words made the record that matters."*
- ⚠️ **Interview the general's staff and copy the order.** Truth +5. *"Accurate — and hollow. An order on paper isn't a day of jubilee."*
- ❌ **Write it from the office; everyone knows what happened.** Truth −10. *"Nobody knew what happened until someone asked. That's the job."*

**Assignment 1 (decision 2 — source check):** *A planter tells you the freedpeople "won't work without masters." Print it?* ✅ Note it as his claim, seek what freedpeople and Bureau agents actually report — print the evidence (Truth +10) · ⚠️ Print his quote with no check (Standing +5, Truth −10) · ❌ Print it as fact in your own voice (Truth −15). *Feedback names the skill: a source with a stake in the story needs corroboration (7.20D/F).*

**Beat matrix:**
| # | Assignment | Reporting beat + source-check beat |
|---|---|---|
| 2 | The Bureau school | Cover the new freedpeople's school and its teacher (✅ show the hunger to learn); check a rumor the Bureau "steals wages" against its actual contract ledgers (✅ documents beat rumor) |
| 3 | The Black Codes | Read the statute and say plainly what it does (✅ name it: forced labor contracts, restricted testimony); a politician offers you his "explainer" — compare his words to the law's text (✅ primary source wins) |
| 4 | Night riders (sensitive) | A freedman will testify about Klan threats **if** you protect his name (✅ protect the source, print the pattern with evidence); the sheriff calls it "exaggeration" — weigh a threatened witness vs. an official denial (✅ corroborate carefully; feedback: courage and care can coexist) |
| 5 | New voters (1869–73) | Cover Black Texans voting and Senators Gaines and Ruby at work (✅ the era's proof of change); check a paper that calls the legislature "ignorant" — spot the loaded words (✅ bias vocabulary, 7.20D) |
| 6 | The 1876 Constitution | Report what the new constitution actually changes (shorter terms, weaker government, school cuts) (✅ effects, 7.5C); closing choice: your retrospective — tell the decade honestly, gains and losses both (✅ the record stands) |

## 5. Higgsfield Assets (standard direction + dignity rules; this game's images carry the most weight)
1 Title: "A young reporter with a notebook on a busy 1870s Galveston street — cotton drays, sailing ships, telegraph wires." · 2 Juneteenth: reuse/echo the Run the Blockade finale — "Galveston, June 1865: joy in the streets as freedom is proclaimed; a reporter writing fast at the crowd's edge." · 3 The school: "A one-room freedpeople's school, students of all ages at slates, a young Black teacher at the board — pride and purpose." · 4 The statute: "A reporter reading a law book by lamplight, the printed page reflected in his glasses." · 5 The witness: "Two men talking quietly at the edge of lantern light behind a church — trust being given, no menace shown." · 6 The capitol: "Black and white legislators at desks in the 1870s Texas capitol chamber — history in session."

## 6. Command Center & Notes
Class-wide accuracy; PDF footer TEKS 7.5C, 7.20D/F. **Sensitivity (the strictest in the catalog):** Klan violence is conveyed through *threat, testimony, and pattern* — never depicted; freedpeople are protagonists with names, courage, and agency, never only victims; the Codes and terror are named plainly (honesty rule) at headline level; the debrief's arc bends toward the long record — Juneteenth's endurance — without pretending the era ended well. Have Fable draft, then review every line of assignments 3–4 against the standards before build.
